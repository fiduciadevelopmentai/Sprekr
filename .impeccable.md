## Design Context

### Users

Sprekr is a free open-source project for people who want fast, private speech-to-text on Apple-silicon Macs. They are not expected to understand terms such as event taps, shortcut recorders, Accessibility APIs, or model runtimes. The core job is simple: choose easy Hold and Toggle controls, speak from any app, and receive text at the current cursor without opening Sprekr.

### Brand Personality

Calm, tactile, and trustworthy. The app should feel private and responsive, with friendly plain-English copy and no technical theatre. Permission or insertion failures happen while the user is already interrupted, so recovery copy must be especially direct and reassuring.

### Aesthetic Direction

Refined organic minimalism: a single calm warm-ivory content field in light mode and a deep green-charcoal content field in dark mode, neutral light or near-black navigation surfaces, monochrome ink-on-light and warm-white-on-dark accents and icons, locally bundled Onest UI typography paired with Crimson Text editorial headings, a consistent subset of Lucide outline icons, and a near-black Flow Bar with warm-white waveform and processing feedback. Fiducia-owned surfaces use the supplied Fiducia mark; Sprekr surfaces use the Sprekr mark. Avoid decorative gradients, colorful shaders, grain, playful multicolor backgrounds, blue or green action accents, shimmer, glow, blur, and decorative drop-shadows. Support System, Light, and Dark appearances. Reference products may inform interaction principles, but Sprekr keeps original code, branding, copy, proportions, and visual details.

### Design Principles

1. The global talk interaction is the hero moment: visible feedback starts immediately and moves smoothly from listening to processing to delivery.
2. Start simple. Say “talk control,” “hold,” and “tap”; onboarding gives Hold and Toggle one clear field each for a keyboard key, combination, or mouse side button. Permission copy explains the outcome before naming the macOS setting.
3. Reserve expressive motion for the final onboarding handoff: flat monochrome motion, progress, tips, then a calm centered completion message without shimmer, glow, or a decorative success icon. Every other transition stays quiet and functional.
4. Stay out of the way. The idle Flow Bar is a narrow handle; it expands only when status needs attention and never steals focus.
5. Never lose words. If no editable field is active, save the transcript, copy it, and clearly say that Command-V will paste it.
6. Motion explains state, uses short natural ease-out timing, remains responsive on the target M4, and becomes static when Reduce Motion is enabled.
